# Research Principles

The Oregon Performance Audit Project (OPAP) is committed to conducting independent, transparent, and evidence-based research on the implementation of Oregon performance audit recommendations.

The following principles guide every stage of the project's development.

---

## Independence

Research is conducted independently of political parties, advocacy organizations, government agencies, and private interests.

Analytical conclusions are based solely on documented evidence and standardized methodologies.

---

## Objectivity

Implementation assessments are grounded in publicly available documentation using predefined coding standards.

Whenever interpretation is required, coding decisions are documented and supported by evidence.

---

## Transparency

Project methodologies, coding manuals, database structures, and version histories are publicly documented whenever practical.

Readers should be able to understand how conclusions were reached.

---

## Reproducibility

Research methods are documented sufficiently to allow another researcher to independently evaluate or reproduce the project's analyses.

Methodological changes are tracked through version control.

---

## Evidence Before Opinion

The project distinguishes between:

- Documented observations
- Analytical interpretation
- Policy discussion

Published findings clearly identify which conclusions are directly supported by evidence and which represent broader interpretation.

---

## Continuous Improvement

The project is designed as a living research initiative.

Methodologies, coding frameworks, and analytical techniques will continue to evolve as additional audits are reviewed and lessons are learned.

---

## Constructive Evaluation

The purpose of OPAP is to promote institutional learning rather than assign blame.

Performance audits are viewed as opportunities for organizational improvement, and implementation analysis is intended to identify patterns that support more effective public administration.

---

## Public Value

Research products are intended to improve public understanding of government performance by making audit implementation information more accessible, understandable, and transparent.

Where possible, findings will be presented in formats that are useful to policymakers, researchers, journalists, students, and the public.
